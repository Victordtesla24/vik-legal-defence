Section 01 — What Happened

The Full Story, in Plain English

Vikram Deshpande is a 40-year-old technology professional with zero criminal history — not a single conviction in his entire life. He has cooperated with Victoria Police as a victim of crime, and even assisted as a witness in a fatal stabbing investigation. He held an average annual income of $200,000 and a 20+ year unblemished career.

It Started with a Redundancy

In March 2025, Vik was made redundant. Like many Australians who lose their jobs unexpectedly, he struggled financially. He fell behind on some fines — small infringement notices that most people pay without thinking. He tried to set up a Centrepay payment plan in June 2025, but Fines Victoria held the application because the fortnightly amount needed a phone confirmation. Some of the fines weren't even his — they were incorrectly allocated infringements that belonged to someone else.

The Suspension That Should Have Lasted One Day

On 18 September 2025, Fines Victoria suspended his driver's licence because accumulated demerit points exceeded the threshold — points that were inflated by the incorrect fines and by unpaid fines that built up while he was between addresses after losing his home following the redundancy.

Vik acted immediately. On the same day — 18 September — he applied for a payment plan to resolve the outstanding fines.

Fines Victoria accepted his action and on 19 September 2025 — just one day later — directed VicRoads (the licensing authority) to lift the suspension.

The Key Fact

The authority that suspended the licence (Fines Victoria) told the authority that manages licences (VicRoads) to reverse the suspension on 19 September 2025. This was one day before the first alleged offence.

But VicRoads' Computer System Couldn't Process It

Here's where the nightmare begins. VicRoads' computer system could not reverse an automatically-triggered suspension, even though Fines Victoria had directed it. When Vik called VicRoads on 26 September (after being pulled over), they made three remarkable admissions:

VicRoads Admissions — 26 September 2025

1. "Our system cannot undo this." — The suspension was automatically triggered and their system physically could not reverse it, even though Fines Victoria had directed the lift.

2. "Your clearance letter is a valid legal document." — VicRoads told Vik that the letter they issued on 24 September was legally valid and he could show it to police if stopped.

3. "Nothing can be done for 3 months." — They said they were "investigating" but the suspension would run its automatic 3-month course (18 September to 18 December 2025), regardless of what Fines Victoria directed.

Three Months of Being Trapped

For the next 91 days, Vik was caught in an impossible situation. Every government agency he contacted acknowledged the error but none could fix it:

Fines Victoria confirmed they had directed the lift on 19 September. VicRoads admitted their system was at fault but said they couldn't override it. Victoria Police kept pulling him over based on their database (LEAP), which still showed his licence as suspended — even though VicRoads had issued a clearance letter.

During this period, Vik filed six formal submissions to Fines Victoria — payment arrangements, enforcement reviews, internal reviews, infringement extensions — a relentless paper trail of someone doing everything in their power to resolve the situation through proper channels.

One police officer, Senior Constable D'Cruze, actually listened. On 13 October 2025, he emailed Vik confirming that if VicRoads showed the licence was clear, he would not proceed with the charge. Vik sent him the VicRoads clearance letter. But on 20 October, D'Cruze emailed again: "your licence is still showing as suspended" in the police database — 26 days after VicRoads cleared it.

The Officer Who Investigated Never Charged Him

SC D'Cruze was the only officer who actually looked into what happened. He received the VicRoads clearance letter. He confirmed the LEAP database was wrong. And then he went silent — no charge sheet was ever issued by D'Cruze, and he never contacted Vik again. This is a Senior Constable making a professional judgment: the charge had no merit. The officers who did charge Vik (Kaynak at Burnley, May at Oakleigh) never investigated at all — they simply saw the database flag and filed charges automatically. The prosecution is now pursuing charges that Victoria Police's own investigating officer rejected.

On 18 December 2025, the suspension automatically expired — like a scheduled computer job completing. No human being intervened. The system just… finished.

Section 04 — Systemic Failure

Three Government Agencies, One Broken System

This is not a case of one mistake by one agency. It's a cascading failure across three separate arms of the Victorian government, each of which acknowledged the problem but none of which could fix it.

Agency 1 — Fines Victoria
Imposed the suspension, then directed it be lifted — but the lift was ignored
Fines Victoria suspended the licence on 18 Sep due to demerit points (partly from incorrect fines). When Vik resolved the issue the same day, they directed VicRoads to lift the suspension on 19 Sep. Their directive was not actioned for 5 days, and even then, VicRoads' system couldn't actually reverse it. Fines Victoria did their part — but the message was lost between agencies.
Agency 2 — VicRoads (Dept. of Transport)
Admitted their system was broken but couldn't fix it
VicRoads admitted on 26 Sep that their computer system physically could not reverse an auto-triggered suspension. They told Vik his clearance letter was a valid legal document. They said they were "investigating" but nothing could be done for 3 months. The suspension would run its automatic course regardless of what Fines Victoria directed. On the very day of the first offence (20 Sep), VicRoads approved Vik's Unregistered Vehicle Permit — proving they were actively processing his applications while failing to process the suspension lift.
Agency 3 — Victoria Police
Database never updated — kept pulling Vik over based on information they knew was wrong
The police LEAP database retained a "Zombie Flag" showing the licence as suspended, even after VicRoads cleared it. On 20 October — a full 26 days after VicRoads clearance — police officer SC D'Cruze confirmed in email that the licence was still showing as suspended in LEAP. Despite holding the VicRoads clearance letter and D'Cruze's own acknowledgment, police continued to intercept Vik based on this erroneous data. The Oakleigh intercept on 3 November was conducted using information that police already knew was disputed.

What does "Zombie Flag" mean?

Victoria Police uses a database called LEAP (Law Enforcement Assistance Program). When a licence is suspended, a flag is added. When VicRoads lifts the suspension, that flag should be removed. In Vik's case, the flag was never removed — it persisted like a zombie, triggering automatic alerts every time his licence plate was scanned by police. Every intercept after 24 September 2025 was based on this dead-but-still-walking database entry.

The Defence — In Plain English

Defence 1 — "Honest and Reasonable Mistake"
The law says you can't be convicted if you honestly and reasonably believed the facts were different

Under Australian law (Proudman v Dayman, 1941), if someone honestly and reasonably believed they were entitled to drive, they have a complete defence — even if their licence was technically suspended. Vik's belief was reasonable because:

• Fines Victoria directed the suspension be lifted the day before the offence

• VicRoads issued a clearance letter confirming the suspension had ended

• VicRoads told him the clearance letter was a valid legal document

• A police officer confirmed in writing that the charge should not proceed

• He filed 6 formal Fines Victoria submissions trying to resolve the situation

Defence 2 — "Diversion" (No Conviction Pathway)
The court can decide to deal with the matter without recording a conviction

Under Victorian law, the court can "divert" a case — meaning the charges are dealt with through conditions (like a good behaviour bond) without recording a criminal conviction. This is appropriate where the accused has no criminal history, the offence is at the lower end of seriousness, and a conviction would cause disproportionate harm. Vik's case ticks every box: zero priors, administrative rather than deliberate offending, and a conviction would destroy his security clearance and career.

Defence 3 — "Unlawful Basis for the Second Intercept"
The police stop on 3 November was based on information police knew was wrong

By 3 November 2025, Victoria Police already had SC D'Cruze's email confirming the LEAP database was incorrect. They had received the VicRoads clearance letter. Yet they intercepted Vik again based on the same erroneous database flag. Under the law (DPP v Kaba, 2014), evidence obtained through an unlawful intercept can be excluded from court. If the stop was unlawful, the drug charges that flowed from it may also be excluded.

What the Prosecution Must Prove (and Why They Can't)

The prosecution must prove beyond reasonable doubt that Vik knew or ought to have known his licence was suspended.

But by the date of the first offence (20 Sep), the authority that imposed the suspension had already directed it be lifted. VicRoads later told him the clearance letter was valid. A police officer agreed the charge shouldn't proceed. Six formal government submissions show extraordinary diligence. And the only police officer who actually investigated the matter decided NOT to charge him. The prosecution's case requires the court to accept that a person who did everything right — who resolved the issue the same day, who was told by VicRoads his letter was valid, who a police officer agreed shouldn't be charged — somehow "ought to have known" he was suspended. That is an unreasonable standard.
